Construction efficiency is vital to real property.

Construction Efficiency Principles

1.

An increase in efficiency will tend to accelerate

redevelopment – makes sense, in that it will be relatively

cheaper to provide a replacement building.

2.

Greater efficiency will lead to a higher proportion of

capital being combined with land, as capital, in the form

of buildings, is made cheaper relative to land –

development is more intensive.

3.

Given no change in the overall demand for

accommodation, greater intensity will tend to produce

increased land values in the centre of the urban region

compared with the periphery.

Demand & Supply Conditions

in the Construction Industry

Special Aspects of Construction Demand

•

Clients individual specification for buildings requires a bespoke

or tailor-made product from supply side.

•

Building durability ensures circa 50% of industry output is in

repair and maintenance sector – products last but have needs.

•

Products used are mainly capital goods, expensive relative to

income, purchased through borrowed funds. Demand is

dependent upon the cost of credit. High interest rates therefore

hurt the industry.

•

Subject to seasonal and cyclical fluctuations – vulnerable to

private sector fluctuations in demand, expectations, rise in cost

of borrowing or changes related to level of income.

•

Government policy, taxation and subsidies – circa 25% of the

industry product is for public sector – note impact of austerity on

expenditure on capital projects nationally and locally.

Distinctive features of Supply

•

As an assembly industry, its operations are consecutive

whereas in manufacturing industries operations are

concurrent.

•

Work is carried out on site – impact of weather, material

storage, moving labour & equipment and work supervision.

•

Minimal technical unit is small – so vertical disintegration.

•

Labour costs form a high proportion – 33-50%* of total

project costs.

•

Time lag between response of supply to a change in

demand regarded as a built-in mechanism to produce short

term fluctuations in activity.

•

Payments to suppliers are in stages, subject to specific

contract provisions – interim, monthly, on completion.

Negotiated contract

•

Tendering usually relies on separation of design &

construction functions (Jowsey, p.255) but not always.

•

Asset delivery (construction) could be just as acceptable

(functionally, aesthetically) and at a lower price if

delivered by a contractor through a Design & Build (D&B)

contracts with one contractor) - large-scale economies

may occur, should result in keener prices for the client.

Speculative Builders

Resemble manufacturers – produce buildings (houses) in anticipation of

demand & when demand is rising.

Selling price of houses (units) determines the bid price for the land.

Development process takes time.

This price paid for the land is important - expensive at time of boom – relatively

cheap in recession!

However rising house prices, with other fixed costs, will increase economic rent

on the land and thus super-normal profit – hence speculative nature of sector.

House prices can be volatile! Supply time lag makes it a risky business –

builders usually undertake this in conjunction with normal main contract work.

Structure of the

Construction Industry

Structure of UK Construction Industry

•

Contractors – large & small (see EU definition of SME’s).

•

Small firms pre-dominate: <25 employees cover 96.8% of all

firms (Jowsey, p.287) but account in value for only 29% of

total work.

•

Demand is characterised by relatively small contracts,

individual in nature, demand fluctuates, disproportionately

high overheads, underutilised specialist equipment.

•

Supply also favours small firms, supervision & management

control difficulties, vertical dis-integration through sub-

contractor use and consecutive nature of work.

•

Small business owners accept lower returns; rate of

bankruptcies in construction twice that of other industries.

on manufacturers prices varies between 30 – 50%.

NI Construction Industry Outlook 2023

CEF Survey June – December 2022

•

Looking at 2022, 50% said their profit margins had worsened or

significantly worsened in comparison to 2021. Over the same

period, 20% had seen an improvement in profit margins

•

62% of respondents do not expect their annual turnover to

increase in 2023. Indeed, some 43% are looking to consolidate

their business over the coming 12 months – with 27% looking

to increase profitability

•

67% of firms said they were either at full or almost full capacity.

Only 7% currently had capacity for significant levels of new

work

•

Materials availability challenges have receded – 80% saying

any issues are now manageable

•

Inflationary issues remain – 63% of respondents said these

were continuing to pose serious financial concerns and 85%

said they expect material costs to continue to rise

NI Construction Industry Outlook 2023

CEF Survey June – December 2022

•

On existing public sector supports – since the introduction

of PAN 01/21 in August 2021, at least £21.5 million has

been paid out to firms on existing public sector contracts

to help with inflationary pressures and materials delays

•

On future public sector projects - 81% wanted to see

schools and healthcare facilities

sector opportunities in Northern Ireland in 2023. Some

60% see the local market declining further in activity

•

Four main challenges, in order, into 2023: Increased cost

of materials; Inflation; Access to skilled labour and;

Political uncertainty/no functioning NI Executive

Labour in the UK Construction Industry

People & Employment

•

Total labour >2m; 67% skilled craftsmen; 88.5% male.

•

Features – one-man band, labour-only subcontractors,

high rate of unemployment & low productivity per worker.

•

Labour only sub-contracting predominates with problems

– tax evasion, lack of apprentices, lack of coordination &

collaboration with other sub-contractors.

•

Rates of redundancy in autumn & winter!!

•

High labour turnover; only key workers retained.

•

Frictional unemployment occurs, region by region.

•

Structured unemployment exists – due to long term

changes in demand or introduction of supply side

innovation / techniques.

Productivity in the

Construction Industry

Productivity in UK Construction Industry

•

Increased productivity = given output achieved at lower cost –

supply curve shifts to right (improved machines, training of

workers) = rise in the Marginal Revenue Product (MRP) = in

theory, wage increases for employees.

•

Increases in labour productivity in the industry lag behind those

in the economy – reason is low level of mechanisation.

•

Slower growth in industry’s productivity as the real cost of

building tends to increase relatively to costs in general.

•

Capital is also not a good substitute for general labour or skilled

workers.

•

Tendency towards self-employment since 1970 has hindered

productivity increases (Winch, 1998).

Innovation & Standardisation

in the

Construction Industry

Standardisation

•

No real opportunity to create ‘mass demand’ through

advertising - as in manufacturing or retail sector.

•

Mass demand for many buildings in some sectors not there

•

Recent trends towards ‘system building’ – use of off-site

construction, modern methods – design, fabrication,

construction in the factory then delivered to site.

•

‘Industrialised building’ has failed to gain an advantage over

traditional methods (Jowsey, p303).

•

Open systems – ‘off-the-peg’ components to achieve

design to meet client requirements.

•

Closed systems – one manufacturer who provides all

components in factory and also erects on site.

Standardisation

Reasons for lack of standardisation

•

Materials account for 50% of the cost of a building, labour

33% - therefore a 25% increase in material cost requires a

37.5% saving in labour cost to break even!! (Jowsey, p304)

•

Off-site construction increases transport costs.

•

Higher capital outlay for cranes for lifting.

•

Product inflexibility – no modifications possible for change

of client requirements – can be achieved but is expensive.

•

Elimination of competition – only a few suppliers for

particular products/solutions – monopolies, both buyers and

sellers – deal done on relative buying strengths.

•

Traditional construction now makes use of factory

components through ‘dimensional coordination’.

Technologies Contribution

Tackling Shortages – Hadrian X

•

Robotic bricklaying system invented by Australian company FastBrick.

•

FastBrick have been continually developing Hadrian X since 2006. Currently the robot

has a laying output of 1000 bricks per day. The human average is 300/400.

•

In 2018 Hadrian X completed unassisted construction of a 180m2 three-bedroom, two-

bathroom house in two days using a variety of brick sizes and laying techniques.

•

Hadrian X completed its first outdoor structure in February 2019, operating through high

winds and low light scenarios whilst achieving accuracy.

Kitchens & Bathrooms

Summary

•

UK construction industry 7% of GDP; employs >£2m people.

•

Efficiency important to real property – impacts upon construction

and maintenance of buildings / assets.

•

Small firms & high amount of casual labour gives impression of

inefficiency but demand conditions are unique; supply conditions

(consecutive operations, on-site production, small technical unit

size, high labour costs, time lags) also very distinctive.

•

Unique interaction of demand and supply side through a wide

variety of procurement / tendering arrangements.

•

Output per worker could be increased by labour incentive

initiatives and substitution of capital for labour but skilled labour

vital for industry – many small jobs are labour intensive.

•

‘Mass manufacturing’ has not been possible or fully embraced –

but innovation through BIM, technology, new supply chain

management approaches, lean construction, greater use of off-

site manufacturing / construction could improve productivity.
