Supply

▪

We saw last week how the demand curve shows what

consumers are willing and able to purchase at each and every

price, all other things being unchanged.

▪

This is only one part of the market, concerned with buyers.

▪

We have yet to consider the role of sellers (i.e. businesses

which could include estate agents, development companies,

construction firms) – this involves an analysis of supply and

the supply side of the industry.

Introduction to Supply

▪

The supply of a product is the amount that producers are

willing and able to produce at each and every price, all other

things being unchanged (Gillespie, p. 107)

e.g. The supply curve might show how many houses a

construction firm would be willing and able to produce at

different prices.

▪

The supply curve is usually upward sloping (bottom left to top

right); therefore, a higher price is usually needed for firms to

be willing and able to produce more.

▪

As the price increases, it becomes more appealing / feasible

to produce more units.

Features of a Supply Curve

▪

Businesses usually need a higher price to cover the extra

costs of producing extra units.

▪

An increase in price (from P1 - P2) will usually lead to an

increase in the quantity supplied (from Q1 - Q2), known

as an extension of supply (Gillespie, p. 106):

Movement along a Supply Curve

If, on the other hand, there were a fall in the price (from P1 -

P2), this would result in a fall in (contraction of) the

quantity supplied (from Q1 – Q2)

Movement along a Supply Curve

A shift in supply means that the curve moves to the right or

left, meaning that more or fewer products are supplied at

each and every price (Gillespie pp. 110-1)

Shift in supply curve

▪

As we saw in the case of demand, the principles are:

-

A change in quantity supplied is caused by a change in price; whereas

-

A change or shift in supply is caused by a change in other factors

.

▪

These other factors include:

▪

A change in the number of producers – if there is an increase in this

number, it would lead to an increase in supply, i.e. an outward shift in the

curve – to the right.

▪

A change in technology – new, better technology should enable firms to

produce more output at any price, thus also shifting the curve to the right.

(e.g. online trading, in construction modular and off-site building, BIM)

▪

A change in costs – An increase in wages / price of materials would mean

that firms could not supply as much at a given price because of higher

costs – they will need a higher price for any given level of output. Thus the

curve would shift to the left (e.g. price of oil, transportation costs, etc).

Factors that cause shift in supply

▪

These other factors also include:

▪

A change in indirect taxes. (Gillespie pp. 110-2). If a tax, such

as VAT, is levied on a good this will increase the selling price

of any given output (as producers seek to recover this

additional cost through higher prices).

e.g in relation to construction; a landfill tax would increase

construction costs and reduce supply at each and every price

(cause curve to shift to left). Other statutory health and safety

legislations which are perhaps costly to the industry, might

have a similar effect.

▪

A change in weather conditions. This can be relevant in

certain markets, such as agriculture / construction where

there are market exposures to changes in the natural

environment. Poor weather can lead to supply shocks which

shift the supply curve to the left.

(Gillespie p. 113). The industry supply curve is the total

supplied by all the firms in the industry at each and every

price, derived from the horizontal summation of all the firms’

▪

The slope of a supply curve will depend on how sensitive

it is to changes in price (Gillespie p.116)

▪

The price elasticity of supply measures the extent to

which the quantity supplied in a market varies with a

change in price:

Price elasticity of supply

= % change in quantity supplied

% change in price

As in the case of demand, the two key elements to understanding

elasticity of supply are

:

-

The sign of the answer (usually positive; price increase

→

increase in quantity

supplied)

-

The size of the answer, which measures the strength of the relationship (if >1,

then supply is elastic, if <1 inelastic)

Price Elasticity of Supply

▪

The formula tells us the relative amount by which quantity

supplied will change in relation to price changes.

▪

For example, if there is a 10% increase in house prices and

this leads to a 1 per cent increase in the quantity of housing

supplied, the price elasticity = (1/10) = 0.1. This is a very small

response so the supply would be considered to be relatively

price inelastic.

▪

As in the case of demand, there are three broad categories:

-

Price-inelastic supply: Where the value <1, as in example above supply is

said to be ‘inelastic’.

-

Price-elastic supply: Where the value >1, supply is said to be elastic;

occurs where % change in supply is larger than % change in price.

-

Unitary-elastic supply: Where the value = 1, a % change in price would

lead to an identical % change in quantity supplied – unlikely in reality.

Price Elasticity of Supply

Extreme cases of price elasticity of supply

▪

The example given to demonstrate price elasticity outlined

a scenario where a 10% increase in house prices was

associated with a 1% increase in the quantity of housing

supplied.

▪

This is a hypothetical example. However, supply is likely

to be relatively price inelastic within the construction

industry because:

- Barker Review (2003) suggested figure of 0.5 for UK

construction industry

- Supply is fixed in short term

- Lags exist due to time involved in site identification,

acquisition, negotiating planning permission, construction…

- Time is main determinant of price elasticity……

‘In the Real World’

Example just considered helps demonstrate how elasticity can vary over

time (Gillespie p. 118): Over a longer period of time, resources can be

more easily shifted from one sector to another, in the short term at least

one factor of production is fixed, restricting firms’ ability to respond to

price changes

Elasticity of Supply over time

The value of the price elasticity of supply for a product will

also depend on the number of firms in the industry -

more firms enable a higher degree of responsiveness to

change in price levels.

Cost of Production

“the sum of all expenses necessarily incurred in the process

of creating and delivering a product or service”

Expenses can include raw materials, labour, equipment,

rent, overhead and marketing costs.

What is Cost?

Total Costs

Fixed costs, variable costs and other costs.

Fixed Costs

Costs that do not vary (increase or decrease) with output.

Variable Costs

Costs relating to how much (the quantity) of a product that

is produced (e.g raw materials)

Marginal Cost

The cost of producing an extra unit of the product or service

What Types of Costs?

Myers, p.98

Fixed, Variable and Total Costs

Myers, p.98

Short Run Costs

Short run cost curves tend to be U

shaped because of diminishing returns.

In the short run, capital is fixed. After a

certain point, increasing extra workers

leads to declining productivity.

Therefore, as you employ more workers

the marginal cost increases.

Because the short run marginal cost curve is sloped like this, mathematically the

average cost curve will be U shaped. Initially, average costs fall. But, when

marginal cost is above the average cost, then average cost starts to rise.

Marginal cost always passes through the lowest point of the average cost curve.

Summary of ATC, AVC & MC

Myers, p.98

•

ATC (Average Total Cost) = Total Cost / Quantity

•

AVC (Average Variable Cost) = Variable Cost / Quantity

•

AFC (Average Fixed Cost) = Fixed Cost / Quantity

Average Total, Variable & Fixed Costs

Fixed Costs & Average Total Cost & Marginal Cost

Fixed costs (FC) remain constant.

Therefore, the more you produce, the

lower the average fixed costs will be.

To work out the Marginal Cost (MC),

you determine how much Total Cost

(TC) has increased by.

For example, the third unit sees TC

increase from 450 to 500, therefore,

the increase in MC is 50.

The 12th unit sees TC rise from 1,700

to 2,400, so the MC is 700.

Revenue

“the amount of money brought into the company, typically for selling

goods, products or services”.

Business Revenue

“the total amount of money brought in to the company by a company’s

operations, measured over a set period of time”.

Marginal Revenue – central concept in microeconomics

“the additional total revenue generated by increasing product sales by 1

unit or the increase in revenue from the sale of one additional unit of

product”.

Marginal revenue (MR) = Δ TR/Δ Q

If a firm sells an extra 50 units and sees an increase in revenue of

£200. Then the marginal revenue of each extra unit sold is £4.

What is Revenue?

Total, Average & Marginal Revenue

This shows the price (P), quantity (Q),

total revenue (TR) average revenue (AR)

and marginal revenue (MR).

If the firm cuts the price from £7 to £6,

quantity increases to 5. Total revenue

increases by two. Therefore, the

marginal revenue is two.

If the firm cuts price from £3 to £2, total

revenue falls by six. Therefore, the

marginal revenue is -6.

Revenue Maximisation

If a firm wished to maximise revenue, it

can use marginal revenue to guide its

decision. If marginal revenue is positive,

the total revenue is increasing.

If marginal revenue is negative, total

revenue is decreasing.

In this example, revenue is maximised at

a quantity of 5.

Normal Profit

“a minimum level of reward required to ensure that existing

entrepreneurs are prepared to remain in their present area

of production”.

Formula:

Profits = total revenues – total costs.

Economists formula:

Economic Profit = total revenues – total opportunity cost of

all inputs used.

Profit

Myers, p.98

Profit

Profit Maximisation

To maximise profits, a firm needs to

consider both marginal revenue and

marginal cost.

If marginal revenue is greater than

marginal cost, then total profits will be

increasing.

Profit will be maximised at the point

where MR=MC

In the real world, an airline may sell

some last-minute tickets for a very low

price. The marginal revenue may be

quite low. However, the marginal cost of

selling empty seats on a plane is also

quite low.

Relationship Between AR & MR

If the firm is a price taker, its demand curve will be perfectly elastic. In this case, the

marginal revenue will be the same as the price and average revenue.

If the price is £5, then the addition to revenue (MR) for selling an extra good, will

always be equal to the price £5.

In perfect competition, the marginal revenue is the same as the average revenue.

Marginal Revenue in Monopoly Market

When a firm faces a downward-sloping

demand curve, then marginal revenue

will be less than average revenue and

can even be negative.

This is because, if a firm cuts price, it gets

a lower average price but also loses

revenue it could otherwise have made

from selling units at a higher price.

Marginal Revenue & Elasticity

When marginal revenue is positive. It

means a cut in price will increase total

revenue. This means that demand is

price elastic (% change in demand

greater than % change in price).

However, if a firm cuts price and

marginal revenue is negative (total

revenue falls). This implies that demand

is price inelastic. (% change in demand

less than % change in price)

At quantity of 6, where MR = 0, at this

point, PED = 1 (unitary elasticity)

This shows the elasticity of demand for a

straight-line demand curve varies.

Business Finance in Firms

Objectives of the Firm

–

Profit maximisation (role of shareholders, short term v long term, differing

margins, each firm or sectors cost structures & expectations are different).

–

Maximise the return on capital employed (take account of risk and long

term stability).

–

Survival (only a short term objective, not attractive to investors).

–

Long term stability (again, lack of ambition can be unattractive).

–

Growth of profit and assets (but not through reckless new share issue or

raising new finance for short term gains).

–

Satisfactory return for all participants and inputs (coalition of suppliers of

capital, managerial skills, labour, goods, services and customers) – Cyert &

March (1963).

–

Maximisation of shareholders’ wealth (takes account of both risk and

return on investment simultaneously).

Key Issues for Firms

–

Evidence of objectives being established.

–

Conflicts of interests: shareholders v managers.

Framework for Decision Making

Financing and Investment

–

To invest or spend – timing of spending matters to people but not in

the same way.

–

Borrowing/lending interest rate represents the opportunity cost to the

shareholder of making investments in projects.

–

Opportunity cost of finance – the existence of a facility to borrow or to

lend means that those who have cash but do not want to spend have

opportunity to lend at the interest rate as an alternative to investing.

–

Investments in projects must therefore compete with that opportunity

and, to be desirable, produce returns in excess of the interest rate.

Framework for Decision Making

Opportunity Cost of Capital

“the amount of income, or yield, forgone by giving up an

investment in another firm”.

Capital will not stay in firms if the rate of return falls below

its opportunity cost.

Opportunity Cost of Labour

“the amount of income, or yield, forgone by an individual

giving up the salary that they could have earned if they had

worked in another company in a similar type of job”.
